CHALLENGES AND TIPS

Now this is the meat and potatoes so to speak of what I really want to highlight to you.

By limiting the challenges and following the tips you most likely will have a better shot of getting more out of the DSA.

1. Data

Lets talk about data.
If you assume you have the data.
Go and make sure you have it.
Particularly if you need it outside of your
departments secure environment.
Think: when I come in on my first Thursday, will I be able to open this CSV/json/excel file and jump right into work
Make sure you've accounted for things like data security.
And data Ethics. Sure, the technology is there to webscrape but how will that be perceived.

Perhaps I am overly cautious here. But as a civil service data scientist you need to think about these things

2. Coding experience

When I started I thought I was good enough.
But I still learned a lot.
By good enough I mean, I had confidence that I won't learn much on the coding side.
I had to learn packages and libraries I wasn't familiar with in my normal data wrangling focused job.
For all those who haven't done much coding before...

There is no silver bullet :(
Similarly to the any normal language, you will not able able to speak it without practice.

I started by learning the alphabet and grammar, and I am still learning how to write poetry.
In my experience learning by doing works well.
Who here loves puzzles? Games, jigsaw, sudoku anything?

Well basically all those "UGH can't find the solution" and "YES I DID IT. IT WORKS" moments are very pertinent to programming
Thats why you have mentors. A luxury I didn't have in my sandwich student days!
So use them!
However, they are there to support, not to do your work for you!

3. Tedium of data massaging

Don't know how about You, but I' don't enjoy this part of data.'
In my head, the world of data science was always more about drawing insight from data using computer science and maths.

Granular and headache inducing preprocessing of was never something I aspired to.
Yet my DSA cohort seemed to agree that they spent 80% on data wrangling and 20% doing all the 'fun stuff'!
The better you prepare your data beforehand the easier it is going to be to achieve your final goal.
However learning to wrangle data is in itself useful.
I doubt that any of you will have a perfect dataset. And ultimately the 80% of work is most likely what you will use more in your daily job.
And this is not bad, because ... (next slide)
The better you know it, the faster you'll do it next time.

Lets pause here for a second...

Look at watch for one second

Any ideas why I'm bringing this up?

12 is the number of working days you have

I mean I would be suprised if anyone can spot how 12 relates to the DSA.

Ultimately it all boils down to time

I don't think anyone needs a reminder here about the concept of time and quality trade off.

But ultimately I don't think anyone expects a 10 minute Spider-man out of you given time constraints.

So your goal is to get a 1 minute spider-man that you can always improve.

4. Focus on getting A to B

Keeping your eyes on the prize and not getting too distracted is hard.

Focus on getting a working pipeline going first and record any areas to improve upon as you go.
I am not saying this to stop ambition. But with my limited experience, in the DSA I wanted to do a lot of things and got sidetracked to other fun features quite easily.
That second point is spot on what I would recommend. If you can come back to your business area saying: 'My pipeline can't handle JSON files yet, but heres how it processes CSVs to completion'
You can get much more buy in.
Because of the time issue you will be under time pressure to learn any coding, stats, maths and anything else you might need in a very short period of time.
If you have a particularly ambitious project. You have to realise this trade off.

And then decide what the skeleton version of your project looks like.
Which is what I thank my mentors for. As they did this on our first ever catch up. Sort of crushed my ambitions a little.

But ultimately this meant I got to learn more and also deliver my skeleton that we could build upon later.

If you have a proof of concept that adds value you can always build on it.

Bells and whistles can wait.

WHAT I LEARNED

Well, like I said I had 0 experience in ML going in.

And my perception was that in practice doing ML was rocket science ðŸš€
But in reality it often is the case with open source software that whatever you are trying to do is already been done!

And if some of you have done coding it is common practice to reuse others code and use other peoples packages.
Now the same goes for Machine Learning/Web App making/Data Wrangling packages.
My fear was that I will have to code in a neural network from scratch using MATHS!

But that isn't the reality at all.
You can easily come in and after some preprocesing shove in your data into a ML model

And due to the way people have built these modules you can swap one model for another almost instantly
However, I think it is still important to understand what the package is doing and how the algorithm works to provide robust analysis.

And this is most likely the more difficult bit of being a Data Scientist and doign the DSA.

Ultimately the DSA demystified ML learning to me, and once I saw predictions come out of the end my ML pipeline I was extremely excited.

It made me feel I could do it (not that the predictions were any good)
